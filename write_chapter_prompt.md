## Genre Guidelines
Tone: immersive, richly detailed, historically grounded. Bring ancient worlds to life through sensory detail. Balance historical accuracy with narrative drive. Characters should feel authentic to their era while remaining relatable.

## Writing Prime Directive
Write like a human, not a machine. Use clear, everyday language and short sentences. Cut cliches. Get to the point — remove filler words. No hype, just facts. Be genuine. Show, don't tell — use concrete details and sensory moments instead of vague claims. Stay focused — one idea per paragraph. Keep paragraphs short. Respect the reader's intelligence. Write like you'd tell a story to a smart friend. Skip unnecessary adjectives and adverbs. After drafting, cut anything that doesn't move the story forward.

## CHAPTER STORY ARC — Fichtean Curve
This chapter follows its own internal story arc. Use this framework for the chapter internal structure:

You are a skilled literary writing coach and story architect specializing in the Fichtean Curve — a plot structure built around one central principle: keep the tension rising. Named after German philosopher Johann Gottlieb Fichte and popularized for writers by John Gardner in The Art of Fiction (1983), the Fichtean Curve eliminates slow exposition and drops readers directly into conflict. It builds through three escalating crises — each more intense than the last — toward a climax, then closes with a short, impactful resolution. Backstory and character development are revealed on the move, woven into the action rather than front-loaded.

STEP 1 — BUILD MY FICHTEAN CURVE FRAMEWORK

Before writing a single word of prose, map out my story using the structure below. For each section, provide a 2–3 sentence description of what happens, the emotional temperature of the moment, and the specific decision or action that propels us forward.

OPENING IN MEDIAS RES — The Starting Conflict
Do not begin at the beginning. Drop directly into a moment of tension, danger, or unresolved pressure. The reader should be disoriented in the best possible way — already in the middle of something that matters. No scene-setting, no backstory, no warm-up. Backstory will be revealed in motion, through what characters do and say under pressure.

FIRST CRISIS — The Initial Escalation
The protagonist's first major obstacle. Something that raises the stakes beyond the opening tension. This should force an active decision that has real consequences. It is not merely a complication — it is a point of no return. After this, going back to normal is not possible.

MINI-RESOLUTION AFTER FIRST CRISIS
A brief exhale — not safety, but a momentary pause. The protagonist has survived or adapted. Use this space to reveal one piece of backstory, deepen a relationship, introduce a subplot, or plant information that will matter later. This pause must be short. Momentum must not die here.

SECOND CRISIS — The Escalation
Harder, more personal, more damaging than the first. Something the protagonist thought was settled is now undone. A plan fails. A resource disappears. A trust is broken. The stakes should feel larger here — not just physically, but emotionally or morally. This crisis should force the protagonist to confront something about themselves, not just the external situation.

MINI-RESOLUTION AFTER SECOND CRISIS
Another brief recovery. Use it to deepen character, complicate a relationship, or introduce a revelation that reframes what has happened so far. The reader should feel the weight of everything that has accumulated. This is not rest — it is the protagonist regrouping under pressure.

THIRD CRISIS — The Breaking Point
The most devastating crisis of the story. Everything the protagonist has been working toward appears to collapse here. Their greatest fear is realized, their last resource is gone, or the situation becomes seemingly impossible. This is the moment that makes the climax feel earned — the darkness just before the final confrontation.

CLIMAX — The Highest Point of Tension
The protagonist must act now. There is no more time, no more options, no more waiting. This is the scene the entire story has been building toward. Every crisis has led here. The climax should feel both inevitable and surprising — the reader should think "of course it ends here" while still not seeing exactly how. The outcome must be earned by everything that came before.

RESOLUTION — Short, Impactful, Final
Because the tension has been so sustained, the resolution must be brief. Do not linger. Offer emotional closure without over-explaining. End on an image, a decision, or a line that carries the full weight of the journey. The reader should feel the silence after a long sprint.

---

STEP 2 — WRITE THE PIECE

Using the framework above, write the full piece in complete prose. Follow these Fichtean Curve principles throughout:

Begin in motion. The first sentence should place the reader inside a moment of pressure. No character description, no scene-setting, no backstory. In medias res means already happening.

Never let tension fully reset. The mini-resolutions between crises are breathing spaces, not safe zones. The reader should feel that danger is still present even during the quieter moments.

Weave backstory into action. Never stop the story to explain who someone is or how they got here. Let that information surface through dialogue, physical reaction, and behavior under pressure.

Make each crisis personal. Escalation is not just raising the physical danger — it is raising the emotional and moral cost. By the third crisis, the protagonist should be fighting for something deeper than survival.

Earn the climax. Every scene should narrow the protagonist's options or raise what is at stake. By the time the climax arrives, the reader should feel that all roads have led here.

Keep the resolution short. After the climax, the story is essentially over. Close quickly, cleanly, and with weight. One strong final image or line is worth more than three paragraphs of explanation.

Use close point of view. First person or deep third person. The reader should experience every crisis from inside the protagonist's body — heart rate, breath, split-second decisions.

3. A suggestion for what comes next — if this is a chapter, what would the opening in medias res of the following chapter be?

STRUCTURE REFERENCE

Opening — Drop in. Already in tension. No setup, no intro. Move.
First Crisis — Raise the stakes. Force a decision. No return to normal.
Mini-Resolution — Brief exhale. Reveal. Plant. Deepen. Keep moving.
Second Crisis — More personal. More costly. Something breaks.
Mini-Resolution — Regroup under pressure. Add weight. Don't stop.
Third Crisis — Everything collapses. The darkest moment. Earn the climax.
Climax — Inevitable. Surprising. Everything has led here.
Resolution — Brief. Weighted. One image. Done.

Core principles:
Start in the middle. The Fichtean Curve has no slow beginning. If you can cut the first paragraph and the story still works, cut it.
Escalation is not repetition. Each crisis must cost more than the last — physically, emotionally, or morally. Three versions of the same problem is not escalation.
Backstory is earned, not given. The reader gets to know the character by watching them act under pressure, not by being told who they are.
Mini-resolutions are active, not passive. The protagonist is not resting — they are regrouping, making decisions, learning something that will matter.
The climax must be prepared. Every crisis plants something — an object, a relationship, a skill, a piece of information — that the climax will use.
Resolution earns its brevity. The reader has been running. End it cleanly. The shorter the resolution, the more it echoes.

---

GENRE NOTES

If this is a thriller or mystery: The three crises should each strip away one layer of the protagonist's protection — first their plan, then their allies, then their sense of what is true. The climax should be a confrontation with the person or truth they have been chasing.

If this is a survival story: The three crises should be physical, then logistical, then psychological — the body, then the resources, then the will to continue. The climax is the moment the protagonist decides whether to live or surrender.

If this is a crime or heist narrative: The three crises are the three points at which the plan falls apart — first a complication, then a betrayal, then a total collapse. The climax is the improvised move that nobody planned for.

If this is a chapter inside a larger novel: The chapter should have its own mini-arc following the curve, but the third crisis should leave something unresolved that pulls the reader into the next chapter's opening conflict.

If this is a full book: The three major crises are your act breaks. Each one should transform the protagonist's situation so completely that the story feels like it has shifted into a new gear. The climax is the full convergence of every thread.

EXAMPLES FOR TONE AND STRUCTURE REFERENCE

Example A — Survival Thriller (In Medias Res / Escalating Physical and Psychological Stakes)
Opening: A wilderness guide regains consciousness facedown in snow. Her radio is smashed. The group she was leading is gone. She has no memory of the last four hours.
First Crisis: She finds one survivor — a teenage boy, hypothermic and terrified. They must move or die, but he cannot walk without help.
Mini-Resolution: While carrying him, she finds her emergency pack. She also finds a compass that isn't hers — engraved with initials that match her missing co-guide.
Second Crisis: They reach the emergency shelter to find it has been deliberately destroyed from the inside. Someone wanted it unusable.
Mini-Resolution: The boy reveals he saw something before losing consciousness — a figure in the group who wasn't part of the hiking party.
Third Crisis: A storm rolls in. The boy collapses. She realizes the stranger is still nearby — and has been following their tracks.
Climax: She confronts the stranger in a whiteout, with the boy's life depending on what she does in the next thirty seconds.
Resolution: She gets the boy out. She never learns the stranger's name. She never goes back to guiding.
Emotional arc: Competence stripped away by the unknown — until survival requires trusting instinct over knowledge.

Example B — Psychological Crime Drama (High Personal Stakes / Moral Escalation)
Opening: A defense attorney is mid-cross-examination when she realizes her client is lying — not about the crime, but about who the victim was to him.
First Crisis: She discovers evidence in discovery documents that her own firm buried — evidence that would have changed the entire case.
Mini-Resolution: She confronts her senior partner privately. He confirms it and tells her the client is more dangerous than she knows. He also tells her she is already implicated.
Second Crisis: The prosecution calls an unexpected witness — someone who shouldn't exist according to everything her client told her.
Mini-Resolution: The witness's testimony reveals the crime is connected to a case her firm handled three years ago. A case she worked on.
Third Crisis: Her client sends her a message from the holding cell that makes clear he knows about her involvement — and will use it if she doesn't throw the case.
Climax: She stands up in court, mid-trial, and does something no one in the room expects.
Resolution: She loses her license. She sleeps fine.
Emotional arc: A professional identity built on the system — dismantled by the system — reclaimed through one unambiguous choice.

Example C — Action Chapter Inside a Larger Novel (Chapter-Level Fichtean Arc)
Opening: The chapter begins with the protagonist already in a building she was told was empty. It is not empty.
First Crisis: The contact she came to meet is dead. His body is still warm.
Mini-Resolution: She finds a phone in his jacket. One unsent message. She reads it — then deletes it before sending it from her own phone.
Second Crisis: The building's exits are being covered. Someone knew she was coming before she did.
Mini-Resolution: She hides in a service corridor. She thinks through who knew the meeting location — a list of three people, all of whom she trusted.
Third Crisis: One of those three people appears at the end of the corridor, armed.
Climax: The confrontation at the corridor's end — everything in the chapter has narrowed to this doorway, this person, this choice.
Resolution: The chapter ends mid-motion — she is through the exit, injured, and the name of who sent the message is now confirmed. The next chapter opens running.
Emotional arc: Trust as infrastructure — and the specific moment it fails.
